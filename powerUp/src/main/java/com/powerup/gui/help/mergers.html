Mergers

Mergers - also known as takeovers - are a crucial part of the game, so much so that being able to initiate the right mergers can be the difference between first and last place.

Initiating a merger

As described in the "Playing a turn" tab, a merger is initiated when a tile is played that is adjacent to at least two different companies' tiles.

If there are exactly two companies with tiles adjacent to the newly played tile, the larger company will take over the smaller one. If there are more than two companies, the largest company will first take over the second largest, then the next largest, all the way down to the smallest.

If two or more companies are the same size when a merger is initiated, the active player may choose which company will survive. If a company tries to take over two or more (smaller) companies which are the same size as one another, the player may choose which one will be taken over first.

Effects of a merger

For each company that merges into the surviving company, bonuses are awarded, shares are sold, and company is liquidated. Each of these stages is detailed further below. Note that in this section, the DoE, if enabled for the game, is considered a player.

Awarding of majority and minority bonuses

Whichever player holds the most shares in the selling company receives a majority bonus of ten times the value of one share at the time of sale, and whichever player (if any) holds the second most shares in the selling company receives a minority bonus of five times the value of one share at the time of sale.

In the event that only one player owns shares in the selling company, they receive the combined minority and minority bonuses.

In the event that two or more players are tied for the highest number of shares in the selling company, they each receive an equal share of the combined majority and minority bonuses. No other bonuses are awarded.

In the event that one player is a clear majority shareholder but two or more players are tied for being the minority shareholder, the tied players each receive an equal share of the minority bonus, and the majority shareholder receives the usual majority bonus.

Here are two examples to help illustrate this process more clearly.

Selling companyManiac Timber
Share price$200
Player 1's ownership 5 shares
Player 2's ownership3 shares
Player 3's ownership3 shares
Player 4's ownership1 share

In the above example, Player 1 receives a bonus of (10 * $200) = $2000 as the majority shareholder, while Players 2 and 3 receive a bonus of ((1/2) * 5 * $200) = $500 each as joint minority shareholders.

Selling companySuperslick Oil
Share price$400
Player 1's ownership 5 shares
Player 2's ownership7 shares
The DoE's ownership7 shares

In the above example, Player 2 and the DoE are joint majority shareholders, so they each receive a bonus of ((1/2) * (10 + 5) * $400) = $3000. Player 1 does not receive a bonus.

Sale of shares

After the bonuses have been awarded, each player sells the shares they hold in the selling company. They receive the value of the shares at the time of sale.

Liquidation of selling company

After each player has sold their shares in the selling company, all of its tiles are transferred to the purchasing company, and the selling company is liquidated. This means they are no longer active, and may be re-created elsewhere on the board on a subsequent turn in the usual fashion (see the "Playing a turn" tab for information on creating companies).
